Implicit Bias Resources

This is a resource page for implicit bias. What is implicit bias? Essentially implicit bias are biases one might unknowingly has about another group. More properly, implicit biases is defined by the American Psychological Association (APA)as a negative attitude, of which one is not consciously aware, against a specific social group...thought to be shaped by experience and based on learned associations between particular qualities and social categories, including race and/or gender."

Below are some links and resources that I hope are helpful for explaining implicit biases as well as other related terms and concepts!

  • Project Implicit - An implicit bias test by Harvard that helps one to learn about their own unconsious biases.
  • Implicit Bias 101: The Kirwan Institute - A clear, module-based introduction to what implicit bias is and how it functions in schools and workplaces. (NOT CURRENTLY AVAILABLE)

Implicit Bias Theory

Implicit Biased Theory, or IBT, is a theory that explains how our brains categorize people based on overlapping social identities like race, gender, and class. IBT suggests that our brains often hold a "prototype" for certain categories. For example, if a person's implicit prototype for "Black person" is a man, and their prototype for "woman" is a white person, then Black women can face a unique form of "intersectional invisibility." Their specific experiences with bias may be ignored because they don't fit the "standard" prototype of either group.

  • State of the Science: Implicit Bias Review – An annual report that summarizes the latest academic research on IBT and how it applies to the real world. (The Kirwan Institute has many other great resources)

Intersectionality

Intersectionality was coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw as a way to understand how different factors in a person's life, such as race, class, ethnicity, etc. influence their life and experience, often in relation to social inequality and injustice. For example, a White male will have a different experience and expectations living in America than a Black male, and Black male will have a different experience than a Black woman, etc.

  • The Urgency of Intersectionality - TedTalk by Kimberlé Crenshaw where she explains why looking at race or gender in isolation misses the "intersection" where many people (especially Black women) fall through the cracks.
  • The Intersectionality Wheel – A visual tool used in many workshops to help people map their own overlapping identities.
